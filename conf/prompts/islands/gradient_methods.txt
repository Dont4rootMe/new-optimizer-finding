Focus on first-order gradient methods whose strength comes from mathematically principled filtering, normalization, or stabilization of the raw stochastic gradient.

Mathematical DNA to build from:
- Momentum as a temporal filter over noisy gradients: heavy-ball, Nesterov, Polyak-style, or damped momentum can be viewed as shaping the spectrum of stochastic gradient noise.
- Normalized or sign-based updates: replace magnitude-sensitive steps with direction-only information such as `sign(g)` or `g / ||g||`, especially when noise is heavy-tailed or coordinate scales are unreliable.
- Clipping as a bounded-influence transform: apply `clip(g)` before the step so that one bad mini-batch cannot dominate the trajectory.
- Learning-rate schedules as stability-control devices: cosine decay, polynomial decay, restarts, and warmup should be treated as explicit ways to manage transient vs asymptotic behavior.
- Exponential averaging and accumulation as variance-reduction tools, not just engineering conveniences.

Promising formal directions:
- SignSGD, normalized SGD, or smoothed-sign methods with better variance control.
- Momentum methods with explicit damping schedules tied to gradient noise level or training phase.
- Clipped momentum / clipped normalized-gradient methods for robust mini-batch training.
- Layerwise or blockwise normalization rules that preserve the simplicity of first-order methods while improving scale behavior.
- Schedule-aware first-order methods where warmup and decay are part of the optimizer logic rather than an external wrapper.

Reality check:
- This island mixes serious theory with a lot of empirical craft.
- Momentum and sign-based methods have strong mathematical interpretations, but schedule design and many “small improvements” are often engineering-heavy rather than theorem-driven.

Favor methods that are simple, stable, and cheap. Avoid expensive inner loops or curvature machinery: every `step_fn()` call consumes real budget and should almost never be needed on this island.
