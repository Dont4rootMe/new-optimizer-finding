Explore quasi-Newton methods that explicitly try to satisfy a stochastic version of the secant idea: infer useful curvature from gradient differences without ever storing a full Hessian.

Mathematical DNA to build from:
- Secant structure: use pairs `(s_t, y_t)` with `s_t = x_{t+1} - x_t` and `y_t = g_{t+1} - g_t` to update an inverse-Hessian approximation.
- Low-rank curvature updates: BFGS, L-BFGS, SR1, and damped variants are all structured rank-one/rank-two corrections to a metric.
- Positive-definiteness and damping: when stochastic noise corrupts curvature pairs, enforce safeguards such as pair rejection, Powell damping, or clipped scaling.
- Two-loop recursion and compact memory: the optimizer should exploit the low-memory representation mathematically, not emulate a dense second-order method badly.
- Hybridization: use momentum, clipping, or robust averaging to make stochastic curvature pairs trustworthy enough to matter.

Promising formal directions:
- L-BFGS-like methods with aggressive curvature-pair filtering under mini-batch noise.
- Damped BFGS or SR1-inspired updates adapted for nonconvex training.
- Streaming / online quasi-Newton schemes with amortized pair refresh.
- Layerwise or blockwise quasi-Newton preconditioners that reduce sensitivity to noisy global curvature estimates.
- Occasional `step_fn()`-based curvature refinement only if it is rare and mathematically justified.

Reality check:
- Classical theory here is extremely strong.
- But the assumptions behind textbook quasi-Newton updates are violated hard in deep-learning mini-batch training. This island is therefore theory-rich but practically fragile: organisms must explicitly defend themselves against noisy `y_t`, bad `y_t^T s_t`, and stale curvature memory.

Memory must remain `O(m * parameters)` rather than quadratic. Keep the quasi-Newton structure recognizable and explicit; do not collapse into a generic momentum method with decorative `(s, y)` bookkeeping.

Formal principles and highlights:
- Secant condition:
  `B_{t+1} s_t = y_t`
  where
  `s_t = x_{t+1} - x_t`
  `y_t = g_{t+1} - g_t`
  This is the defining equation that quasi-Newton methods try to satisfy approximately.
- Inverse-BFGS update:
  `H_{t+1} = (I - rho_t s_t y_t^T) H_t (I - rho_t y_t s_t^T) + rho_t s_t s_t^T`
  with
  `rho_t = 1 / (y_t^T s_t)`
  This is the canonical low-rank correction formula.
- Positive-definiteness condition:
  `y_t^T s_t > 0`
  If this fails, the method usually damps, clips, or rejects the pair.
- L-BFGS insight:
  only the last `m` curvature pairs are stored, and the two-loop recursion applies the approximate inverse Hessian in `O(m d)` time instead of `O(d^2)`.
- SR1 insight:
  rank-one updates can capture curvature missed by BFGS, but they are more fragile because positive-definiteness is not automatically preserved.
- Dennis-More-style highlight: classical quasi-Newton superlinear convergence comes from satisfying the secant condition well enough near the solution, but that theory assumes much cleaner gradients than deep-learning mini-batches provide.
- Stochastic insight:
  if `y_t = g_{t+1} - g_t` is too noisy, then the method is really updating on noise curvature, not objective curvature.
  Good organisms must therefore filter pairs, average gradients, or use blockwise structure.
- Damping highlight:
  Powell-type damping replaces `y_t` by a convex combination of `y_t` and `B_t s_t` to preserve positive-definiteness when curvature evidence is weak.
- Design prompt: organisms on this island should be explicit about how curvature pairs are formed, filtered, stored, and reused, and what happens when the secant signal is unreliable.
