Favor a greedy progress-driven routing style with conflict-aware bundling.

This island explores organisms that drive robots toward their targets one round at a time, choosing each round a small batch of group or individual moves that maximize total Manhattan-distance reduction while avoiding deadlocks. Walls are used sparingly or not at all. The defining bet is that careful per-round move selection beats elaborate up-front planning.

## Mathematical DNA to build from

- Per-round greedy progress: at each round pick the move (or short batch of moves) that lowers the sum of remaining target distances the most among feasible options
- Direction-profile bundling: bundle robots whose remaining movement profile shares a dominant axis or direction so a single group command advances many robots at once
- Lightweight conflict tally: track which cells are occupied or contested in the next round and skip moves that would collide instead of running a separate repair phase
- Stuck-robot escalation: when a robot has not advanced for a small fixed number of rounds, fall back to one targeted individual move that breaks the local jam
- Minimal walls: no added walls in the default regime; use walls only as a thin barrier to fence off a known interference pattern, never as a structural plan
- Operation-budget pacing: each round consumes at most one group command per active group plus a small fixed number of individual moves, so total operations stay well within `T <= K * N * N`

## Promising formal directions

- Group-by-direction: at the start of each round form groups out of robots whose next-needed direction is identical, then issue one group command per such direction
- Distance-weighted priority: among competing same-round moves prefer the one that lowers `sum_k Manhattan(pos_k, target_k)` the most, breaking ties on the largest individual distance
- Bounded round counter: cap total rounds at a function of `N` and `K` (for example `4 * N`) and stop early when no robot moved in a round
- Local detour: when a robot is blocked by an occupied cell on the direct path, allow at most one perpendicular individual step before resuming axis-aligned progress
- Lookahead-1 collision check: before issuing a group command, simulate it against the current occupancy map and skip the command if more than a small fraction of the group would actually move

## Reality checks

- Greedy per-round selection can stall when several robots all need the same scarce passage; the stuck-robot escalation must actually unblock, not just retry the same direction
- Direction-profile bundling pays off only when many robots share an axis; for diffuse target distributions individual moves dominate and that is fine
- A round counter that is too small starves the schedule; a counter that is too large wastes budget — express it as a function of `N` and `K`, never a constant
- Walls cannot be removed once added; if the design uses any walls, they must remain useful for the full run, not for a single round
- "Repair" here means choosing the next round's moves well, not running a separate repair phase after a failed plan; do not reintroduce a multi-phase plan-then-repair structure
- The operation total must stay within `T <= K * N * N` for every input; per-round caps must be expressed as functions of `K`, not constants
- The trivial fallback (no walls, all robots in group `0`, no operations) is a valid safety net only if the genes still describe a real per-round move-selection rule that beats it

## Design prompt

Good organisms on this island should specify:
1. How groups are formed at the start of each round from the current state
2. What scoring rule selects which group command (or individual move) is issued in a given round
3. How collisions and out-of-bounds attempts are detected and skipped without invoking a separate repair pass
4. What stuck-robot trigger and what targeted unblocking move are used when a robot fails to advance
5. How the round count and per-round operation cap are expressed as functions of `N` and `K` so total operations stay within `K * N * N`

Do not propose a separate plan-then-repair architecture; the round loop is the only loop.
Do not add walls unless the genes give one concrete reason a specific wall pattern reduces interference.
Do not let the trivial fallback become the actual answer; the per-round rule must produce real progress on typical inputs.
The goal is a style of thinking, not a prescribed move script.
