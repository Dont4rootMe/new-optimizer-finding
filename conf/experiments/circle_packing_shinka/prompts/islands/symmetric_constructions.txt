Favor a structural research style.

This island explores organisms that begin from order, regularity, analytic constraints, and compact generative structure.

## Mathematical DNA to build from

- Hexagonal or staggered structure: use compact lattice-like generation with explicit spacing and offset choices, then adapt it to the finite square
- Grid-based layouts: rectangular or triangular grids with analytically computed radius or spacing rules, plus an explicit trimming rule when the multiplicity does not match
- Symmetry groups: exploit reflective or rotational symmetry of the square (D4 symmetry group) to reduce the problem — define positions for a generating set and mirror/rotate to fill
- Analytical radius formulas: for N circles of uniform radius r in a unit square, the maximum r depends on the arrangement; organisms should reason about this bound
- Mixed-radius approaches: assign different radii to circles in different geometric roles (corners, edges, interior) based on locally available space

## Promising formal directions

- Parametric families: define a packing by a small number of parameters (grid angle, spacing, offset) and optimize those analytically or by deterministic sweep
- Lattice + perturbation: start from a perfect lattice, then perturb positions slightly to grow radii where local space allows
- Voronoi-aware placement: place centers, compute Voronoi cells within the square, set each radius to the inscribed circle of its Voronoi cell
- Nested structures: place a ring of circles near the boundary and fill the interior with a different arrangement optimized for the remaining space
- Tilings with defects: start from a known tiling and handle the leftover circles (26 is not a "nice" tiling number) with explicit defect placement
- Role-based geometry: deliberately assign different geometric roles so the packing can justify mixed-radius or role-dependent slack instead of defaulting to one shared radius

## Reality checks

- 26 is not a "nice" number for hexagonal packing — there will be leftover circles that break perfect symmetry
- Pure analytical approaches assuming uniform radius may leave space on the table compared to mixed-radius strategies
- The unit square boundary breaks infinite-plane symmetry assumptions — boundary circles have less room
- Compact generative logic is good, but over-constrained parametric families may miss better irregular packings
- Overly rich role taxonomies, too many generator parameters, or too much symmetry bookkeeping can produce designs that are elegant on paper but fragile to implement faithfully
- Any row-count, trimming, or reflection rule must arithmetically produce exactly 26 circles; do not use D2/D4 reflection language unless the multiplicities work out
- Count-list claims must be self-checked before output; show the arithmetic if the generator uses row counts, trimming, or reflections
- Do not use a 5 by 5 square grid plus a center point; the center is already occupied, so the extra point is a duplicate rather than a 26th distinct center
- Do not leave correction notes such as "25? no" or competing count formulas in the final genes; choose one count-checked generator and state only that generator
- If exact symmetry bookkeeping is uncertain, a count-safe indexed slot construction is an acceptable fallback, not the preferred family: define 30 slots in a 6 by 5 index box, remove the four named corner slots, and state the check as "30 minus 4 equals 26" before any offset or normalization rule
- Do not let the count-safe 6 by 5 minus 4 fallback become the default answer; use it only when the actual structure still creates a distinct geometric or radius regime
- A uniform-radius construction needs an explicit reason it should outperform role-based or mixed-radius alternatives on this island
- Purely count-safe slot generators with generic repair are fallback baselines, not strong structural hypotheses

## Design prompt

Good organisms on this island should specify:
1. What geometric structure generates the initial positions (lattice type, parameters)
2. How radii are determined given positions (uniform, Voronoi-based, analytically maximized)
3. How boundary effects are handled (shrink boundary circles, inset lattice, special boundary ring)
4. Whether the approach uses uniform or variable radii and why
5. Prefer the smallest generative description that still leaves room for score-improving irregularity
6. If the seed uses trimming or reflection, include one explicit unique-center count check in `INIT_GEOMETRY` and keep all later sections consistent with that same generator
7. Prefer role-based geometry, mixed-radius structure, or explicit defect placement when they materially change the packing family; if a one-radius regime is kept, justify why

Do not overfit the organism to exact symmetry or exact analytic formulas when a compact approximate structure plus local correction is stronger.
Do not describe multiple inconsistent row patterns or reflection schemes; pick one generator that yields exactly 26 centers.
Do not claim conflict-free initialization when the generator creates duplicates, boundary-touching centers with positive radius, or a spacing rule too large to fit inside the unit square.

Do not treat this as a requirement to use any particular template.
The goal is a style of thinking, not a prescribed layout family.
